# Group Game Axes

AMAZE borrows TIGRIS's lesson that group-game quality is argued through axes,
not hidden inside one aggregate score. During simulation, personas draft axes
and stake claims. The narrated scene/beat run either earns, contests, or refutes
those claims.

## Axis pool

| Axis | Question for an escape room |
|---|---|
| Agency | Do players feel their choices and observations matter? |
| Interaction texture | Does the room create useful table talk, pointing, handing, calling, and coordinating? |
| Parallelism | Can multiple people contribute without fragmenting the story? |
| Teachability | Does the room teach its language before asking for harder uses? |
| Pacing | Do tension, release, discovery, and acceleration happen at the right moments? |
| Fairness | Can players explain why the solution was earned after they see it? |
| Role coverage | Do scouts, scribes, mechanics, faces, and navigators all get useful work? |
| Spectatorship | When one player acts, do others have something meaningful to watch or infer? |
| Replay conversation | Would the team discuss alternate paths, missed clues, or favorite moments afterward? |
| Social safety | Does pressure stay playful rather than embarrassing or coercive? |
| Recoverability | Can the room recover from a wrong path, missed clue, slow team, or failed prop? |
| Signature moment | Is there a moment only this room could deliver? |

## Draft protocol

Before a simulation:

1. Pick three to five personas or roles.
2. Each drafts two axes from the pool.
3. Each stake names a predicted strength or risk.
4. During the narrated run, mark each stake:
   - **earned** when two specific beats support it;
   - **contested** when evidence cuts both ways;
   - **refuted** when two beats contradict it;
   - **silent** when the simulation never touched it.

Silent stakes are design information. They usually mean the axis, persona, or
room concept is too vague to argue.
